Light Pollution

Light pollution, as the name implies is an overuse or misuse of lights and especially at night. While they may seem harmless or absent from our lives, in most developed and semi-developed countries lights are often overused, as seen in the image above. If you have access to the internet, you most likely experience this pollution to an extent. One great way to see just how much light goes into the night sky is with this interactive website of the world map at night here. Things like excessive streetlights, lamps, bright billboards, bright light bulbs and more are examples of light pollution. According to National Geographic, there are 4 main kinds of light pollution; glare, light trespass, clutter and skyglow. Glare is focused horizontal light with blinding health risks (such as a bright street lamp when driving), light trespass is when light is too strong and ends up reaching to areas not meant to be lit (like a lamp post’s light extending into someone’s home), clutter is when there are too many lights, confusing your senses (like the side of buildings in metropolitan areas) and finally skyglow is light bouncing up into the once clear night sky (as seen in the image above). Aside from hurting the environment by wasting energy needed to sustain these unnecessary lights, light pollution also has adverse health effects for humans and animals as well.


Impacts and cause of light pollution:

Light can be a significant factor in human and our ecosystems health. This is because it disrupts and confuses the circadian rhythm, which is responsible for our behaviour, body temperature, mental health and production of hormones. Most commonly it creates a higher risk of sleep deprivation which leads to anxiety, depression, obesity, migraines and seasonal affective disorder. However, in more extreme cases an overexposure to light has been linked to some types of cancer. Hormones such as melatonin are excellent at suppressing and killing cancer cells before they develop but can only be produced with a low light environment when sleeping. So much so that in 2007, the international agency for cancer research listed night work as a “probable human carcinogen”, for its link to breast cancer and prostate cancer patients. Similarly, animals also feel the effects of lights at night. Many insects, birds and frogs are heavily attracted to light, resulting in them being easily preyed upon, in an area they shouldn’t be and/or exhausting them to death. Others feel the opposite effect and are repelled by our artificial light, thus creating a form of habitat loss and forced migration. Both of these kill and boost the populations of certain animals, ultimately resulting in an unbalanced ecosystem and loss of species.

Despite these dangers light pollution is still extremely common across the developed world, but why? you might ask. Well, it’s mostly because of urbanisation and globalisation, when used correctly, artificial lights are extremely helpful in navigation and safety as well as extending our days so we can be more productive. Unfortunately, many light sources in modern times are far too bright, badly designed or placed unnecessarily. The biggest and most common causes are street lamps, stadiums, fireworks, billboards, cars, and more. Luckily, light pollution is one of the easiest pollution to remedy, which are listed below.


What can you do about this?

To reduce the effects of light pollution and reduce its commonality, you can:

"Saving the Dark | Documentary | Light Pollution [HD]."YouTube, uploaded by Sriram Murali, 31/12/2019

  • Try your best to buy IDA approved light fixtures, the International dark sky association values lightbulbs by how much energy they use, their brightness, amount of light created, etc… As the name suggests, this association tries to find the balance between helpful lights and harmful lights.

  • Compact fluorescent and LED lights extend less than other bulb types, thus reducing light trespass, and they are also the most energy effective bulbs.

  • Install timers, sensors and other devices that automatically switch off lights as to not waste electricity and provide light when it’s not needed.

  • Turn off lights when not in use.

  • Use an eye mask if your bedroom has unavoidable light from light trespass pollution. This will reduce the impacts of light when sleeping and is extremely cheap to implement.

  • Get dimmers for lights to reduce the effects of glare and therefore avoid eye damage.

  • Buy warm coloured light bulbs, (more yellow than blue) as blue lights have been shown to damage eyes and have stronger effects of glare. Additionally, blue light has a higher capability of skyglow than any other colours.

  • Remove any unnecessary light sources to save electricity and contribute less to light pollution.

  • Raise awareness about light pollution or teach others around you about its dangers and how to prevent them.

  • Finally, an easier way to reduce light pollution on a larger scale without being a lawmaker, manufacturer of light bulbs, politician etc, is to donate to charities that aim to reduce light pollution of all kinds. A great candidate for this is The International Dark Sky Association, although there are many others.
