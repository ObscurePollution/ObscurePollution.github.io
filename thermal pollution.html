Thermal Pollution

Thermal pollution, otherwise known as thermal enrichment is any kind of pollution that changes the temperature (typically warmer) and in turn the quality of ocean, lake and river water. Thermal pollution can somewhat be thought of as global warming but exclusively for the water, as it is almost entirely man-made as well as affecting temperature. Unlike the other 2 pollutants covered on this site, thermal pollution can’t exactly be attributed to a single reason and is mostly caused by human globalisation.


Impacts and causes:

Speaking of which, here are some of our man-made contributions to thermal pollution. Firstly, factories and machinery frequently use water as a cooling agent, seeing as it's free and ubiquitous everywhere. This process begins with cool water being run through the gears and other moving parts of a machine with warm water coming out and then dumping it back into the ocean. Similarly, sewage and other harmful chemicals are dumped into the seas which are usually warmer than the oceans as well as toxic to its wildlife. Another cause is deforestation, since trees provide an excellent source of shade and cooling from transpiration, when they are cut down bodies of water around them are heated. Also, as you’d expect global warming and its greenhouse gases also increase sea temperatures. Although these are the main factors by humans, there are many others like retention ponds being transferred from into the ocean, discharge from hot roads, and hydroelectricity.

On the other hand, there are also naturally occurring sources of thermal pollution, such as volcanic eruptions, heat vents and soil erosion. Since the first two are self-explanatory, soil erosion causes thermal pollution by rising sea levels and thus giving more surface area for sunlight to take effect. One particular ironic naturally occurring factor is fish dying and too many fish. This is because organic matter when alive creates warmth from movement and respirating and when decomposing releases a lot of the energy in the form of heat and ammonia. These contradictory factors can create a cycle of death by thermal pollution, which is why a balance of wildlife needs to be sustained. Knowing that our seas are massive in volume, it can be hard to determine just how prevalent thermal pollution is, so hopefully the following statistics can put things into perspective.

As you can see, there has been a significant upward trend that shows that oceans across the world have increased by 0.78°C. Another important statistic is the surface of the seas temperature as it can also gauge the rate of climate change, since greenhouse gases only affect the top of the water. This statistic can be seen below.

“Average Global Sea Surface Temperature, 1880–2015”
by United States Environmental Protection Agency.

Even if 1-2 degrees Celsius may seem little compared to our day to day weather and seasonal temperature changes, it is still significant compared to how stable our global temperatures usually are. According to the United States Environmental Protection Agency, sea temperatures have been “consistently higher during the past three decades than at any other time” which is also the timeframe a lot of urbanisation and globalisation took place.

As I’ve been hinting and you’ve probably been thinking, thermal pollution kills and imbalances marine ecosystems. Mainly, this is because warm water holds less oxygen than cool water, an increased metabolism, reproduction issues and toxins as an inevitable byproduct. Because of how fragile the food chain can be, the death or migration of species can result in others dying or growing, but eventually even those who grow in population die out as well. This loss of biodiversity as mentioned before will result in more thermal pollution. While it may seem like they don’t affect you directly, in the long term it will indirectly, through the loss of food sources and increased rates of global warming (which itself has a plethora of consequences).


What can we do about this?

Because of the industrial nature of the causes, it’s hard to do anything about thermal pollution on a small, individual scale. The only way for this is to donate to a thermal pollution/Ocean environmental charities or to support agencies supporting marine life and discourage those who do the opposite or even contribute themselves. However, manufacturers can reduce their effects on thermal pollutants by treating coolant water before dumping it back, recycling cooling water and even founding an artificial pond with no animals in it exclusively for cooling uses.
